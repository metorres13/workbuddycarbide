Pro Tips for Tiempo de Ciclo

We asked veteran machinists and application engineers from Ingersoll, OSG, and Mitsubishi Materials for their best Tiempo de Ciclo advice. Here's what they told us.

1
Start at 70% of the recommended maximum.

A Ingersoll applications engineer gave us this rule: whatever the catalog says, start at 70% and ramp up after verifying chip formation. Modern Mitutoyo surface finish testers will tell you when you've crossed the line.

2
Radial engagement changes everything.

OSG's technical team emphasizes that Tiempo de Ciclo that works at 100% radial engagement won't work at 30%. As engagement drops, chip thinning occurs — and your Tiempo de Ciclo needs to compensate. Mahr tool presetters can help verify your actual engagement before cutting.

3
Check your runout — twice.

Even a Mitsubishi Materials high-performance end mill at optimal Tiempo de Ciclo will perform poorly with excessive runout. A Lyndex-Nikken shrink-fit holder with <3µm runout can extend tool life by 40%+ compared to a worn ER collet.

4
Listen to the cut.

Experienced machinists on Grob and Starrag cells can hear when Tiempo de Ciclo is off. A consistent, crisp cutting sound is your best real-time validation. If it starts to chatter, back off 10%.

5
Document everything.

The best shops we work with keep a notebook next to each machine. Tiempo de Ciclo, tool brand (Ingersoll/OSG/Mitsubishi Materials), coating (Balzers (Oerlikon)), coolant concentration — record it all. When you nail a setup, you'll want to reproduce it next month.
